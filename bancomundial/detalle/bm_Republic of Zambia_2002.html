 0 The miombo woodlands are still relatively intact woodland savannah ecosystem covering 40% of Zambia. The miombo is of outstanding international importance for the conservation of flora and fuana, many of which are endemic to the region. In response to the highly acidic soils of the miombo woodlands, the traditional farming system is the slash and burn where vegetation is removed from between 2-3 ha by farmers and burnt in plots averaging 0.3-0.4 ha. The ashes increase the ph of the soils and are a source of additional nutrients. This system supports a period of cultivation of up to 5 years. However, rapid population growth is increase pressure on land and progressively shortening the period of fallow. Decline in soil fertility has led to food insecurity. The rapid degradation of the soil is also of global concern. The project is intended to promote a shift from slash and burn to more sustainable land management practices which have been successfully tried in other areas. These practices include the application of conservation farming (CF) at the farm level and integrated ecosystem management (IEM). Examples of CF are crop rotation, no- or minimum tillage, use of agricultural lime and the use of crop cover crops. IEM measures includes community fire control and agroforestry. The vehicle that will be used in the promotion of these practices is the succesfully tested Farmer Field Schools.
