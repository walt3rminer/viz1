PNPM?s approach to poverty reduction is not divorced from macro-level factors such as improving the overall investment climate or building larger network infrastructure and service supply systems. But engaging communities directly in localized poverty reduction efforts has already been shown to be an efficient, sustainable way to cover very large, poor populations in ways not otherwise available to public agencies. The potential benefits of the PNPM program are projected to be significant. Some 7-9 million households would be pulled out of poverty. Furthermore, over two-thirds of the expected benefit stream is indirect, the result of the increased economic activity created by PNPM, primarily from improved economic infrastructure, and the importance of those indirect benefits continues to increase in later years. At that point it moves into a sustaining mode. During this period local governments will take on greater responsibility for financing. GOI also expects to bring supply-side initiatives from health, education, rural development and other sectors into the PNPM umbrella to maximize complementarities for poverty reduction.
